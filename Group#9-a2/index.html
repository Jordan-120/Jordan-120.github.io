This site compares three American-made tuner cars

The tuner market has mainly been dominated by Europe and Japan, with major players like BMW and Subaru. America has never been a strong competitor in this market, but that doesn’t mean they haven’t tried. The U.S. produced a few competition-based tuners that won many awards and set lap times on the Nürburgring. The main three are the Cadillac CTS-V, Dodge Neon SRT4, and the Cobalt SS. We have links above that provide a brief description of each vehicle, along with stats and a summary of our opinion.

What is a Tuner Car?

Tuning a car means optimizing it for specific needs and requirements, such as occasional track performance, heavy-duty track use, fuel mileage, or drag strip performance. Some cars are tuned for low reliability but massive performance, while others are tuned for higher reliability and moderate performance. Some cars are tuned for straight-line speed and traction, while others are tuned for cornering and stiffness.

From the factory, everything about the car is tuned for "median" use—primarily, the ability to drive the car daily or regularly, with low vibration or harshness, and as few trips to the mechanic as possible. For the average person, there is usually no better performance than what the car offers in its OEM form.

Engine tuning involves everything from basic performance modifications (such as intake or exhaust systems) and adjusting ECU calibrations to modifying the valvetrain, improving compression ratio, adding boost (air pressure), or injecting substances like nitrous, water, or methanol into the air-fuel mixture.

Suspension tuning is a crucial part of the tuning process and can involve altering suspension settings, adding bracing, or even changing the geometry and configuration of the suspension altogether.
